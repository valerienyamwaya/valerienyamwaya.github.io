A Little About Me

If you had told me years ago that studying Clothing, Textile and Interior Design would lead me into cultural research, community work, heritage documentation, and creative enterprise, I probably would have smiled and gone back to making clothes.

My love for design and creativity led me to Egerton University, where I took the initiative to launch an annual fashion showcase—a platform that gave students the opportunity to display their designs, collaborate with fellow creatives, and engage with the wider creative community. Through organizing these events, I learned valuable lessons in leadership, teamwork, planning, promotion, and event management.

What began as a student project quickly grew into an event that attracted participants from universities across the country. Through showcase themes such as Beauty in Diversity, 50 Ways of Expression—which celebrated Kenya's 50 years of independence—and Untamed Imagination, I unknowingly began to find my voice.

I became increasingly interested in the stories behind the things we make, wear, and share, and in how creativity can be used to express identity, culture, and belonging.

My work at university earned me the opportunity to represent Kenya at the Out of the Vase International Design Competition in Aalsmeer, Netherlands, where I won the People's Choice Award in 2015. That experience opened doors to a number of programmes and fellowships, including the WE CREATE Kenya Women Enterprise Fellowship, where I was named 1st Runner-Up in the Business Model Challenge; Fashion Product Lab by Metta and Hivos; the East African Art Creative Enterprise Programme by the British Council and Nesta; and the Training and Skills for Kenyan Micro-Entrepreneurs Programme, delivered through a partnership between ACTEC and Strathmore Business School.

As the Founder and Creative Lead of Afrivazi, I have spent more than a decade exploring the relationship between culture, creativity, and enterprise. In 2019, I led the transition of the brand into menswear, a move that expanded our reach and attracted clients beyond Kenya.

Through Afrivazi, I have worked with artisans, emerging designers, and creative entrepreneurs, particularly within the informal sector, supporting their growth while helping to tell the stories behind their work.

My commitment to people and community began long before my professional career. Through organizations such as Mwamko Mpya Women Group, Sauti Kuu Foundation, and Stawisha Africa Initiative, I have been involved in programmes focused on literacy, mentorship, youth development, and women's empowerment.

These experiences reinforced a lesson that continues to guide my work today: meaningful change begins with people and the stories they carry.

Whether I am reading with children at the Makina Market Children's Reading Club, supporting community initiatives such as the Apate Music Club in Siaya, visiting art galleries across Nairobi, dancing to Dodo with Luo women, exploring places like Kit Mikayi and the Crying Stone of Kakamega, learning from elders, or documenting cultural traditions alongside a visiting scholar from Italy, I am happiest when I am learning from people and listening to their stories.

Curiosity has taken me to many places, but it is these encounters that continue to shape how I see the world. While I am grateful for the awards and opportunities I have received, I am most proud of the relationships I have built, the communities I have worked alongside, and the stories I have helped preserve.

Today, my work brings together fashion, culture, research, and community engagement. I am curious about how people preserve traditions, express their identities, and imagine better futures. Above all, I believe that culture is not only something we inherit—it is something we actively shape, share, and carry forward.
