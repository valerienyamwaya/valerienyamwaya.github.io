I've spent years in conversations about sustainability, particularly within fashion and the creative industries.

We talk about sustainable fibres, circular economies, responsible consumption, ethical production, waste reduction, and designing for a better future.

But over the last few years, I've found myself asking a different question.

What if many African communities have been living sustainably for generations, long before we had a name for it?

Over the last year, my curiosity has taken me beyond fashion. I've wandered through galleries, attended live music performances, visited heritage sites, sat with artisans and market traders, listened to musicians, spent time with community groups, and gradually came to the realization that sustainability isn't always something new waiting to be invented. More often than we realise, it has quietly existed for generations, woven into everyday life in ways we rarely stop to recognise.

Fashion was the first place I noticed it.

If you grew up in Kenya in the late 1980s or 1990s, chances are your mum had a sewing machine, an aunt had one, or there was a neighbour everyone knew who could sew. Sewing wasn't necessarily someone's full-time job. It was simply a skill many people had. Garments were repaired, altered to fit younger siblings, and made to last. Fabrics were reused. Clothes were passed between friends and family, often changing hands several times before they reached the end of their life.

My parents often tell me that in the 1970s and 1980s, borrowing clothes for weddings, celebrations, and special occasions was common. Today we might describe it as rental fashion or a sharing economy. Back then, it was simply what people did. The currency wasn't money—it was trust, relationships, and goodwill.

I also remember my mother making clothes for us from leftover pieces of fabric she had used for other outfits, together with material salvaged from older garments, carefully combining colours and patterns to create something entirely new.

Looking back, it strikes me that many of us grew up around practices we would probably call sustainable today, even though nobody used that word. It was simply how people lived

he same questions followed me into music and heritage, but most importantly, into knowledge transfer.

During one of my visits to a Nyatiti-making workshop in Siaya, I watched young people learning from an elder who has spent decades making instruments and teaching others. They spoke about traditional trees becoming harder to find and the need to adapt by using alternative materials.

They also spoke about another change. Today, anyone willing to learn—including girls and women—is encouraged to do so. Traditionally, the instrument was taught only to carefully chosen apprentices by Nyatiti maestros. The craft is evolving, not by abandoning tradition, but by adapting so it can continue.

I realised I wasn't simply watching the making of an instrument but knowledge being passed from one generation to another, people adapting to changing environments, continuity and in many ways, sustainability in action—long before anyone in that space used the word. it wasn't the first time I had seen this, but it was one of the clearest examples.

What has become increasingly clear to me is that communities have been adapting, repairing, sharing, teaching, preserving knowledge, and sustaining livelihoods for generations. Yet the realities of African communities are often far more nuanced than the global frameworks we use to describe them, and they are rarely placed at the centre of important conversations.

What's interesting is that these observations aren't just based on memory. They also appear in earlier discussions about Kenya's informal economy. In The Informal Sector Workshop, Volume 2: Mobilisation of the Informal Sector for Kenya's Development (1986), the informal sector is described as having significant potential for growth, with production characterised by repair, improvisation, and making use of available materials and tools. Reading it today, I couldn't help but notice how closely those descriptions resemble many of the conversations we now have about sustainability and circular economies.

Perhaps the challenge isn't that these systems don't exist.

Perhaps we've simply become better at measuring some forms of sustainability than recognising others.

That thought has led me to yet another question that I don't think we ask often enough.

As we talk about sustainability, what about sustainable incomes?

Having worked alongside people in both formal and informal creative economies, I've noticed something that continues to bother me.

A customer buying from a formal business will often accept the stated price without much hesitation. The same customer may negotiate extensively with an artisan, tailor, basket weaver, or craftsperson working in the informal sector until the price barely reflects the value of the work.

If the people in the informal creative economy can't earn sustainable incomes, can we really call the system sustainable?

Sustainability shouldn't stop at products. It should also be about people and whether people can continue working without sacrificing their livelihoods. Whether the next generation sees value in learning skills passed down to them because they offer not only cultural meaning but also economic independence.

Have we been looking for sustainability in the wrong places?

Maybe the answers are found in workshops, markets, community libraries, music, storytelling, and conversations with people whose experiences rarely shape the language we use to describe development.

Perhaps it's time we began asking different questions.

Before introducing new solutions, have we fully understood the systems that already exist?

Before designing policies, do we have representation that cuts fairly across all sectors?

Before discussing sustainability, are we giving equal attention to sustainable livelihoods?

And before we define what should be preserved, have we asked communities what they believe is worth carrying forward?

I don't yet have answers to these questions.

But I hope to continue exploring these ideas, listening to the people who live them every day, and being part of conversations that help shift the narrative.

I believe some of the most important conversations about our future begin by paying closer attention to the people, practices, and knowledge that have been quietly shaping our communities all along.
